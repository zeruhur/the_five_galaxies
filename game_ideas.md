Given the 24XX chassis, games where you can define 4-6 specialties, one
supplemental mechanic, and a table set — these translate cleanly:

An espionage game — characters are intelligence operators working in the gaps
between polities. The supplemental mechanic is a Cover die: starts at d10,
steps down when you attract suspicion, breaks when it hits d4 and someone gets
burned. Specialties map to tradecraft roles: Handler, Field Agent, Analyst,
Tech, Infiltrator, Cover Specialist. The GCP's institutional politics,
Skopalian monitoring stations, and the Ethereal Enclave are all ready-made
threat sources.

A ruins survey game — characters are contractors entering pre-collapse sites
(Xalith Rift, Celestial Dominion megastructures, Elysian vaults). The
supplemental mechanic is a Site Clock: a shared countdown that advances on
failures and setbacks; when it fills, the site becomes inaccessible or
hostile. Specialties: Surveyor, Xenolinguist, Security, Engineer, Medic,
Pilot. One-shot friendly by design.

A grey-market operations game — characters work for or around the Void
Consortium running cargo, brokering access, and moving things that polities
can't officially move. The supplemental mechanic is Heat: accumulated per job,
triggers complications between missions, cleared by spending credits or
burning a contact. Specialties: Pilot, Negotiator, Slicer, Muscle, Broker,
Fixer.

A clan drama game — characters are Hetsu clan members navigating Confederacy
politics, Xalith access disputes, and Imperial border pressure. The
supplemental mechanic is Clan Standing: a shared reputation track that rises
and falls collectively. Specialties tied to clan roles rather than
professional skills.

The strongest immediate candidates are the espionage and ruins survey games —
both have clear mission structures that suit 24XX's one-page design
philosophy.
